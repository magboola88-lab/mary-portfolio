The Landing Page Copy Mistakes Quietly Costing You Customers

By Mary Agboola  ·  Conversion Copy  ·  6 min read

Your landing page is getting traffic. The channel is working. The targeting is right. But people land, scroll for a few seconds, and leave without doing anything.

Before you change the design, adjust the layout, or bring in a developer — look at the copy. Because in the vast majority of cases, the problem is not how the page looks. It's what the page is saying.

The headline problem

Most SaaS landing page headlines describe a product. "The modern project management platform." "AI-powered analytics for growing teams." "Cloud-based CRM with advanced automation."

These headlines are categories, not value propositions. They tell visitors what type of thing you are. They don't tell visitors what changes when they use you.

The question a headline needs to answer is not "what is this?" It's "what is different about my situation after I use this?" Compare: "Advanced HR software with integrated compliance tools" versus "Stop losing hours to HR admin — and never miss a compliance deadline again." One describes a product. The other describes a before and after. Visitors respond to the before and after because that's how they think about their problem.

"The visitor is not thinking about your product. They are thinking about their problem. The headline that speaks to their problem wins."

Features are not benefits

The second most common mistake is writing about what the product has instead of what the customer gets.

Features are real-time collaboration, advanced reporting, and 50-plus integrations. Benefits are "your team is always working from the same version," "you can prove ROI to your CEO without pulling a report manually," and "connects to the tools you already use without a migration project."

Features require the reader to translate — to figure out what the feature means for their actual day. Benefits skip that step and land directly on the outcome. Most visitors won't do that translation work. They'll just leave.

Social proof that proves nothing

"Great product, highly recommend" is not social proof. It's a sentence that could appear on a review of a kitchen appliance.

Social proof earns trust through specificity. Who said it? What role? What specific result did they see? How quickly? The more a potential customer can see someone like themselves in a testimonial, the more it functions as real evidence. A quote from a VP of Marketing at a 60-person SaaS company describing a specific outcome does far more work than five-star ratings with no context.

The one-goal principle

Every additional option on a landing page splits your visitor's attention. Every nav link is an exit. Every secondary CTA competes with the primary one. Every "also check out" section leaks the visitor you just paid to acquire.

Landing pages that convert well have one goal. One action the visitor is being asked to take. Everything on the page serves that single decision — and anything that doesn't serve it gets removed, regardless of how useful it feels to have it there.

Give people one clear path. Make that path obvious. Then get out of their way.
