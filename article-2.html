How to Do a Content Audit That Actually Tells You Something

By Mary Agboola  ·  Content Strategy  ·  7 min read

The phrase "content audit" gets used constantly in marketing conversations. It usually means one of two things: a spreadsheet listing every URL on the site with nobody sure what to do with it, or a vague recommendation to "repurpose old content" that never gets acted on.

Neither is an audit. Neither tells you anything you can act on.

A real content audit answers one specific question: why is this content not doing what it should be doing — and what exactly needs to change? Here is how I approach it.

Start with the data, not the content

The biggest mistake in content audits is starting by reading your content. Don't. Start with your analytics.

Pull twelve months of data from Google Analytics and Search Console. Sort by organic sessions, then by conversions. These two lists will not look the same — and the gap between them is the most important information you have.

Your highest-traffic pages that convert poorly are attracting people at the wrong stage of the funnel or mismatching their intent. Your lower-traffic pages that convert well are doing something right that deserves to be understood and replicated. Before you read a single word of your own copy, you want to know which pages are working, which ones are wasting traffic, and which ones have never had a real chance.

"The goal of an audit isn't to find what's broken. It's to understand why — so you can build something that actually works instead."

The four questions every page needs to answer

Once you have the data picture, go through your content against four questions:

Does this page match the search intent behind its target keyword? If someone searches a comparison query and lands on a generic overview, they leave. Intent mismatch kills pages that are otherwise well-written.

Is this page comprehensive enough to compete? Thin content rarely ranks for competitive terms. Check what the top three results for your target keyword look like. If they're 2,000 words and yours is 500, you already know what needs to change.

Does this page give the reader a clear next step? Every piece of content should move someone somewhere — a related article, a resource, a contact form. Content with no next step leaks pipeline quietly.

Would a reader learn something they couldn't find elsewhere in five minutes? If the honest answer is no, the page isn't earning its place in your content program.

What to do with what you find

The output of a good content audit is not a list of things to delete. Most content problems are fixable. The action categories I use are: expand and optimise, consolidate with a similar page, redirect traffic to a stronger page, or start fresh.

Pages worth expanding are those sitting in positions 8 to 20 in search — they've proven relevance but aren't delivering yet. Expanding the depth and fixing the intent match usually moves them.

Consolidation applies when you have three articles covering variations of the same topic and none of them rank well. Combine the strongest elements into one comprehensive piece, redirect the others to it, and let one strong page do the work of three weak ones.

The audit isn't the end of the work — it's the beginning. What it gives you is a clear picture of exactly where to invest your effort for the highest return, rather than guessing and publishing into a void.
